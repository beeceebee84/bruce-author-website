The Night We Burned the Boats

A story about irreversible decisions and the freedom found in having no way back.

Bruce C Bee • June 15, 2025
Fiction Adventure 15 min read

The water was black that night, like liquid obsidian swallowing the reflection of our torches. Marcus stood knee-deep in the shallows, gasoline can in hand, his face flickering orange in the firelight. "Once we do this," he said, voice barely above a whisper, "there's no going back."

I remember laughing—the kind of laugh that starts deep in your belly and claws its way up your throat. The kind that feels more like an exorcism than amusement. "That's the whole fucking point," I told him.

We'd been planning this for months. Six leaky fishing boats stolen from the harbor, hauled up onto this deserted stretch of beach under the new moon. Enough supplies to last us two weeks if we rationed, which we wouldn't. The island wasn't going anywhere, but our resolve might.

Marcus splashed gasoline across the nearest hull. The acrid smell mixed with salt air made my eyes water—or maybe that was the weight of what we were about to do. Behind us, the others had already begun chanting, their voices rising with the flames as the first boat caught fire.

"There are moments when you can feel your life bifurcating—like time itself is a river forking around some immovable stone. This was one of those moments."

By dawn, all that remained were blackened ribs poking from the shallows like the bones of some sea monster. The tide would claim them soon enough. We stood there watching, the heat of the fires still warming our faces, until the sun rose over the island we could no longer escape.

Funny thing about burning boats—it's not the act itself that changes you. It's waking up the next morning with singed eyebrows and the absolute certainty that every decision from here forward matters more than the ones before.

The First Night

No one slept that first night on the island. How could we? The adrenaline hadn't worn off, and every snapping twig sounded like retribution coming for us. Elena kept the fire going while the rest of us took turns standing watch, though what exactly we were watching for, none of us could say.

It was Javi who broke the silence around 3 AM. "You think they'll send search parties?"

Marcus snorted. "For stolen boats? In this economy?"

But we all knew that wasn't what Javi meant. The question hanging in the salty air was whether anyone would come looking for us.
