AFTERMATH

Jah-Bliss

Heat, hunger, and the morning after — a city that calls you by name.

Content note: Intimacy and mature themes. Explicit details have been softened for readability.

Chapter Three

Upstairs, the air changed. Music thinned to a heartbeat while laughter blurred into a hush. It felt like stepping through a curtain — into a room where want had its own weather.

Jo glanced back with that half-dangerous smile. “Yuh feel it?” she whispered. “Follow mi.”

The night didn’t rush us. It unfolded. Fingers laced, breath close, a slow choreography of courage and invitation. We didn’t need instructions — just the rhythm already moving through our bodies.

Some doors you open with a key. Others open because you finally stop pretending you don’t want what’s behind them.

By the time we hit the street again, the night had shifted from velvet to iron. At the Airbnb, the driver leaned on his hood, lips tight. “Five t’ousand.”

Jo inhaled slow, measuring the fire. “Every time mi pay yuh di same,” she said, voice low enough to cut. Bills slapped his chest, sharp as flint. The moment cracked — and the city exhaled with us.

Hunger dragged us sideways to a roadside drum. Smoke rose like a hymn — pimento, sweet heat, steel on cutting board. The cook smirked at Jo. “Yuh late tonight.”

Jo dipped a finger in pepper sauce and pressed it to my lips, eyes bright with dare. Sweet first. Then the slow blaze. I swallowed, jaw locked. She laughed like she already knew I’d refuse to fold.

Back inside, steam gathered on tile and mirror. We learned the language the night had been teaching us — one breath at a time. No witnesses. No rush. Just the kind of silence that leaves a mark.

There are scenes the page doesn’t need. The body remembers them anyway.

Morning slid in on the TV’s low murmur. Jo curled on the couch, rolling a spliff with the patience of a tide. Sunlight climbed her skin into gold. She felt me looking.

“Mawnin’.” A lazy smirk. I answered, and the room warmed again.

We talked about home — mine fractured, hers rooted. I told her how it felt to land in a place where everyone looked like me. She nodded like she was mapping a constellation I only just noticed.

“Di real Jamaica is a survivor,” she said. “We loud when we happy. Loud when we vex. We feel everything.”

She came back with escoveitch snapper — crisp skin, vinegar bright as a bell, peppers singing lead. Rice and peas, cabbage, plantain. I took a bite and could only say, “Damn.”

Some meals are more than food. They are permission.

The hill into town was all heat and shouting brakes. Montego Bay met us with vendors, basslines, and the perfume of fried dough. Voices bloomed from doorways: “Fimi!”

Jo didn’t slow. She traded jokes, paid Miss Patsy, stole a mango smile from the fruit man. The name followed her like a chorus.

“So… Fimi,” I tested. She side-eyed me, grin tucked away. “Yuh haffi earn dat.”

At Miss Ling’s, steam rose from bowls built to fix the soul — chicken soup with extra spinners. “Mi nuh water down seasoning fi nobody,” she warned. We laughed because the truth tasted good.

Jo flagged a route taxi and the city blurred into an upstairs shop — clippers buzzing, children weaving, cats sun-drunk in doorways. A stylist combed my roots and nodded like a verdict. “Mi fix dis up proper.”

I watched Jo at the window, silhouette cut from light. The city didn’t just belong to her. It answered when she called.

“Out of many, one people.” Some days the motto isn’t a phrase — it’s a pulse under your skin.

By evening, the day had stitched itself into me — pepper heat, roadside smoke, a city that renamed us both. I didn’t know how long I’d stay. I only knew I wasn’t done listening.
